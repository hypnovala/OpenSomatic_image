Title
Why Your Body Stays Braced After a Long Hospital Shift

Hook (<=12 words)
If you can’t “relax,” your body might still be protecting you.

Reel Beats (5–7)
1) Pattern: “You’re home, but your shoulders are still up.”
2) Explain: bracing = protective muscle tone when the system expects demand.
3) Why it happens: alarms, urgency, unpredictability → your brain predicts “more.”
4) Add nuance: stress hormones can drop slower than your shift ends.
5) The loop: tension becomes the body’s default “ready position.”
6) Reset micro-practice: 30-second “unbrace” (exhale + soften jaw + drop shoulders + open hands).
7) Close: consistency beats intensity—do it at transitions (car, doorway, shower).

On-Screen Text (3 lines)
1) “Why you stay braced after shift”
2) “Your body learned: be ready.”
3) “Exhale → soften → signal safety”

Caption (100–160 words)
After a long hospital shift, it can feel like your mind is off the clock… but your body is still “on.” Shoulders up. Jaw tight. Belly braced. That’s not you failing to relax—it’s your nervous system doing protection.

In high-demand environments, your brain learns to predict “more coming”: more alarms, more needs, more urgency. Bracing is the body’s ready position. And stress chemistry can take longer to settle than your shift does.

Try this 30-second unbrace at transitions:
• Long exhale
• Soften jaw + tongue
• Let shoulders drop
• Unclench hands

Repeat at the car, the doorway, or before bed. Small signals, repeated often, teach safety.

Education only, not medical advice.
